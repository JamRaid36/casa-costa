The Vision

The House We're Building

Not a smoke shop. Not a bar with a humidor bolted on. A room built the way the best rooms always have been — around the hour, not the clock.

Picture leather worn soft by other men's evenings, low brass light that never asks you to see the time, and a cedar-lined room at the back where the air itself smells like patience. Casa Costa is being built in Florida on that one idea: that a great cigar deserves a great room to be smoked in, and a great room deserves people who are in no rush to leave it.

There will be a bar, but it will not perform for you. There will be a humidor, but it will not be a display case — it will be a working room, kept at the temperature and humidity a serious blend actually needs. There will be chairs arranged for conversation, not for photographs.

Some rooms are built to impress. This one is being built to be lived in.

Casa Costa isn't open yet. It's still a floor plan, a wine-dark paint chip, a stack of research on wrapper leaf and relative humidity, and a promise to a city with more cigar history than almost anywhere else in the country. What follows is the dream in full — the reserve, the pairings, the lineage it's built on, and the room itself, waiting for its first light.

The Reserve

What We Keep in the Cedar

Long-filler, hand-rolled, and nothing shortcut. A well-made cigar tells you before you ever light it.

A good roll gives a little under the thumb without going soft. The draw should feel like pulling a milkshake through a straw — resistance, not struggle. Light it evenly and a well-constructed cigar repays you with a firm, pale-grey ash that holds its own shape. That is the standard the Reserve is built on: a torcedor's hands, whole leaves, no shortcuts in the filler.

A Note on Puro

When every leaf in a cigar — wrapper, binder, and filler — comes from a single country, it's called a puro. No blending across borders. It's the purest expression of one piece of ground, and the Reserve keeps a rotating selection of them for exactly that reason.

The Vitolas

Vitola is the correct word for a cigar's size and shape — never "type." Ring gauge is measured in sixty-fourths of an inch: fatter rings smoke slower and cooler with more room for a blend to unfold; thinner rings run faster and lead with the wrapper.

Corona
5.5″ × 42–44
The benchmark parejo. The size every other size is quietly measured against — balanced, unhurried, built for an actual hour.
Robusto
5″ × 50–54
Short and thick. Faster into full flavor, dense smoke, the working man's classic reimagined as an American favorite.
Toro
6″ × 50–54
The most poured size in the country for a reason — a full ring gauge with enough length to actually finish an evening.
Churchill
7″ × 47–50
Named for the man who never seemed to be seen without one. A long, generous smoke for a long, generous conversation.
Lancero
7″+ × 38–40
Thin and long — the purest test of a blend there is. Master rollers make these first, to prove what a leaf can do.
Torpedo / Belicoso
6–6.5″ × 52
A tapered head that concentrates smoke and flavor to a point. Houses draw the line between the two differently — ask your roller which they mean.

The Wrappers

Connecticut Shade
Mild
Grown under cheesecloth tents for a pale, silky leaf. Creamy, nutty, a whisper of cedar — the easiest morning smoke in the house.
Maduro
Process, not plant
Extended fermentation turns any leaf dark — cocoa, coffee, molasses. Dark does not mean strong; strength lives in the filler underneath, not the wrapper's color.
Habano
Spiced
Leather, earth, black pepper on the finish. A wrapper with some teeth to it, without tipping into harsh.
Corojo
Peppery
A sharper, spicier kick than Habano — a wrapper that announces itself early and holds the line all the way down.

Pairings

What's in the Glass

The logic isn't flavor-matching — it's weight-matching. Pair the body of the pour to the body of the smoke, and let the two argue a little.

Bourbon
The Universal
Vanilla, caramel, oak. If you don't know what a cigar wants, hand it a pour of bourbon — it almost never argues.
Rye
Sharper
More bite, more spice. Built for a peppery Corojo or Habano wrapper that isn't looking for a gentle partner.
Islay Scotch
Peat & Smoke
Laphroaig, Lagavulin — reserve these for a full, earthy cigar that can stand up to a genuinely smoky pour without disappearing.
Aged Rum
Caribbean Kin
Versatile and forgiving, echoing the same molasses sweetness a good maduro already carries. Shared heritage, shared table.
Cognac
Elegant
Dried fruit and oak in a lighter register — built for a medium-bodied cigar you actually want to taste, not fight through.
Espresso
Morning House
A Connecticut-wrapped smoke and a short, dark pour is the closest thing the Reserve has to a daytime ritual.
Port
After Dinner
Sweet, heavy, unhurried — poured last, alongside a maduro or oscuro, when the evening has already decided to run long.
Heritage

The Lector's Chair

Before Casa Costa, there was Ybor City — and before Ybor City decided what a cigar house could be, there was a chair no one was allowed to sit in.

In the 1880s, Vicente Martínez Ybor moved his cigar operation from Key West and Havana to a stretch of scrubland outside Tampa. Cuban, Spanish, and Italian immigrants followed the work, and within a few decades the neighborhood that took his name had become something the rest of the country hadn't seen before: by the 1920s, more than two hundred factories and roughly twelve thousand workers were hand-rolling something like half a billion cigars a year. Tampa didn't ask to be called the Cigar Capital of the World. It just was one.

Here is the part that should give you chills. The rollers on those factory floors — men and women doing exacting, repetitive work for ten or twelve hours a day — pooled their own wages to hire someone whose only job was to read to them. He sat above the floor on a raised platform called la tribuna, and from open to close he read the news, political tracts, and serialized novels the workers themselves had voted on. No one there needed to be taught to love a story. They built a chair for it.

The lectores held real cultural authority — enough that in 1931, factory owners, spooked by the role they'd played organizing strikes, banned them outright. Radio filled the silence not long after. But the idea the lector left behind never really left the leaf: a cigar house, at its best, has never just been about the smoke. It's a room built for gathering. For voice. For somebody always having a story worth stopping to hear.

Pull up a chair. Someone always has a story.

This ground never really stopped being cigar ground. Oliva — still one of the most respected names in the business — began rolling in Tampa in 1934, a few miles from where the lectores once read. Casa Costa doesn't claim to be the first chair in that room. Only that it remembers whose room it was, and intends to keep the tradition of the reader — the gathering, the story, the hour nobody rushes — very much alive.

Membership

A Room, By Invitation

Casa Costa will not be a walk-in humidor. It will be a small number of Spanish-cedar lockers, held under your name, in a room kept the way a serious collection deserves to be kept.

Your Locker

A private drawer lined in Spanish cedar, aromatic oils and all, held under your name in the house humidor — not a shelf, not a shared bin.

69–72% RH

The house target, held year-round. Not a rough estimate — the actual range a serious collection needs to age the way it's supposed to.

Plume, Not Mold

A well-aged cigar sometimes develops plume — a flat, dry, crystalline dusting of oils that wipes clean. It's a good sign, not a bad one. Mold is fuzzy, raised, and never welcome. Know the difference before you panic.

Past the Sick Period

Freshly rolled cigars carry a harsh, ammonia-heavy edge for the first three to twelve months. Serious houses rest their inventory past it — the same patience you'd extend a good bottle of wine.

Membership will be extended by invitation, in small numbers, as the room comes together. If you'd like to be considered, request an evening.
